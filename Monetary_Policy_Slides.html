1. Monetary Policy Overview

Monetary Policy is conducted by a central bank (like the Federal Reserve in the US) to manage interest rates and the money supply.

  • The Ultimate Goal: Influence aggregate demand to achieve price stability (low inflation) and maximum employment.
  • The Core Mechanism: Central Bank actions → Change in Interest Rates → Change in Interest-Sensitive Spending (Investment & Consumption) → Change in Aggregate Demand.
Critical AP Exam Note: When answering FRQs, never skip the "interest-sensitive spending" step! You must explicitly state that lower interest rates increase investment/consumer spending, which *then* shifts AD.

2. Money & The Banking System

To understand Monetary Policy, we must understand money creation.

  • Functions of Money: Medium of exchange, Unit of account, Store of value.
  • M1 Money Supply: Currency in circulation + Checkable (demand) deposits. (Highly liquid).
  • Fractional Reserve Banking: Banks only hold a portion of deposits in reserve and loan out the rest, effectively "creating" new money.
  • The Money Multiplier: 1 / Reserve Requirement. This calculates the maximum expansion of the money supply from a new deposit.

3. Two Frameworks: Limited vs. Ample Reserves

The AP Exam now requires you to distinguish between two distinct banking environments. This is a highly tested area!

  • Limited Reserves (Traditional): Banks hold very few excess reserves. The Central Bank must actively shift the money supply to change interest rates.
  • Ample Reserves (Modern): Banks currently hold abundant excess reserves. Traditional tools don't work well, so the Central Bank directly changes administered interest rates.

Read the FRQ prompt carefully! It will explicitly state which system the economy is operating in.

4. The Money Market (Limited Reserves)

Used ONLY when the prompt specifies a "limited reserves" system.

  • Money Demand (Md): Downward sloping. Inverse relation between nominal interest rates (opportunity cost of holding cash) and quantity demanded.
  • Money Supply (Ms): Vertical line set by the Central Bank.

5. Traditional Tools (Limited Reserves)

In a limited reserves system, the Fed uses these tools to shift the vertical Money Supply (Ms) curve.

  • Open Market Operations (OMOs): The most common traditional tool.
    • Buy Bonds: Equals "Bigger Bucks". Increases Ms, lowers interest rates.
    • Sell Bonds: Decreases Ms, raises interest rates.
  • Discount Rate: The rate the Fed charges banks to borrow money. Lowering it increases Ms.
  • Reserve Requirement: Lowering the required % banks must hold increases the money multiplier and Ms.

6. The Reserve Market (Ample Reserves)

Used when the prompt specifies an "ample reserves" or modern system.

  • Notice the Supply of Reserves (Rs) intersects the Demand (Rd) on the flat, horizontal portion. Shifting Rs left or right here does not change the interest rate!

7. Modern Tools (Ample Reserves)

Because shifting the money supply doesn't change interest rates in an ample system, the Fed relies on Administered Rates.

  • Interest on Reserve Balances (IORB): The primary tool. This is the interest rate the Fed pays banks for keeping money in their vaults.
  • By moving the IORB up or down, the Fed shifts the entire horizontal floor of the Reserve Demand (Rd) curve up or down.
  • The Trap: Never suggest "buying or selling bonds" to fix an economy if the prompt says "Ample Reserves." The answer is always changing the administered rate (IORB).

9. Loanable Funds Market connection

Don't confuse the Money Market with the Loanable Funds Market!

  • Money Market: Determines the Nominal Interest Rate. Driven by the central bank and the demand for cash.
  • Loanable Funds Market: Determines the Real Interest Rate. Driven by national savings (supply) and borrowing by businesses/government (demand).
  • Fisher Equation: Real Interest Rate = Nominal Interest Rate - Expected Inflation.
