1. Welcome to Macroeconomics

Macroeconomics is the study of the entire economy as a whole. While microeconomics focuses on individual consumers and firms, macro focuses on aggregate (total) metrics.

  • Core Issue: Scarcity (unlimited wants vs. limited resources).
  • Three Major Macro Goals: Economic Growth, Low Unemployment, and Low Inflation.

2. The Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)

The PPC shows the maximum combinations of two goods an economy can produce given full employment of resources.

  • Economic Growth: Shifts the entire curve outward.

3. Demand and Supply

The foundation of market economics. The interaction of buyers and sellers determines prices.

  • Equilibrium (P*, Q*): Where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.

4. Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

GDP is the total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a given year.

  • Expenditure Approach: GDP = C + I + G + (X - M)
  • C = Consumer spending, I = Investment, G = Government spending, X-M = Net Exports.
  • Does NOT include: Intermediate goods, non-market transactions, or used goods.

5. Nominal vs. Real GDP

It's crucial to adjust for inflation to see true economic growth.

  • Nominal GDP: Measured in current prices (unadjusted for inflation).
  • Real GDP: Adjusted for inflation using base-year prices. This is the best measure of economic growth.
  • GDP Deflator: (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) x 100.

6. Unemployment

The unemployment rate measures the percentage of the labor force that is jobless and actively seeking work.

  • Frictional: In between jobs / searching.
  • Structural: Skills don't match available jobs (technological change).
  • Cyclical: Due to a recession / downturn in the business cycle.
  • Natural Rate of Unemployment (NRU): Frictional + Structural (Cyclical is 0).

7. Inflation and the CPI

Inflation is the general increase in price levels, decreasing purchasing power.

  • Consumer Price Index (CPI): Measures the cost of a "market basket" of goods bought by a typical consumer over time.
  • Formula: (Price of basket in current year / Price in base year) x 100.
  • Causes: Demand-pull (too much money chasing too few goods) and Cost-push (negative supply shock).

8. The Business Cycle

The recurring ups and downs of Real GDP over time.

  • Expansion: Real GDP grows, unemployment falls, inflation may rise.
  • Peak: The highest point of an expansion.
  • Contraction (Recession): Real GDP falls for at least 6 months, unemployment rises.
  • Trough: The lowest point before recovery begins.

9. Aggregate Demand (AD)

AD is the total demand for all final goods and services in the economy at various price levels.

  • Downward sloping due to: Wealth Effect, Interest Rate Effect, and Foreign Trade Effect.
  • Shifters: Changes in C, I, G, or Net Exports (X-M).

10. Aggregate Supply (SRAS & LRAS)

Total production in the economy.

  • Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS): Upward sloping. Input prices (like wages) are sticky. Shifts via changes in resource prices, actions of gov (taxes/regulations), or productivity.
  • Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS): Vertical line at full employment. Represents the economy's potential output.

11. Macroeconomic Equilibrium (AD/AS Model)

Where Aggregate Demand intersects Short-Run Aggregate Supply.

  • Recessionary Gap: Equilibrium is to the left of LRAS (high unemployment).
  • Inflationary Gap: Equilibrium is to the right of LRAS (high inflation).

12. Fiscal Policy

Actions taken by the Government (Congress/President) to stabilize the economy.

  • Expansionary (Fix a recession): Increase Government Spending or Decrease Taxes.
  • Contractionary (Fix inflation): Decrease Government Spending or Increase Taxes.

13. The Spending Multiplier

Initial changes in spending lead to further changes in spending and income.

  • Marginal Propensity to Consume (MPC): How much of extra income people spend.
  • Marginal Propensity to Save (MPS): How much they save. (MPC + MPS = 1)
  • Spending Multiplier Formula: 1 / MPS or 1 / (1 - MPC).
  • Tax Multiplier: -MPC / MPS. (Always 1 less than spending multiplier).

14. Money and Banking

Money is anything commonly accepted as payment. The banking system creates money through loans.

  • Functions of Money: Medium of exchange, Unit of account, Store of value.
  • M1: Currency in circulation and checkable deposits (most liquid).
  • Reserve Requirement: The % of deposits banks must keep in the vault.
  • Money Multiplier: 1 / Reserve Requirement.

15. The Money Market

Shows the demand and supply for money.

  • Money Demand: Downward sloping (Inverse relation between nominal interest rate and quantity of money demanded).
  • Money Supply: Vertical line determined by the central bank (Federal Reserve).
  • Interest rates are determined by where these two curves intersect.

16. Monetary Policy

Actions taken by the Central Bank (Federal Reserve) to alter the Money Supply and influence interest rates.

  • Tools: Open Market Operations (buying/selling bonds), Discount Rate, Reserve Requirement.
  • Expansionary ("Easy Money"): Buy Bonds, Lower Discount Rate, Lower Reserve Req. (Increases AD).
  • Contractionary ("Tight Money"): Sell Bonds, Raise Discount Rate, Raise Reserve Req. (Decreases AD).

17. Loanable Funds Market

The market where savers supply funds to borrowers.

  • Supply of LF: Driven by national savings (public and private).
  • Demand for LF: Driven by private investment and government borrowing.
  • Crowding Out: Government deficit spending increases demand for LF, raising real interest rates and reducing private investment.

18. The Phillips Curve

Shows the relationship between inflation and unemployment.

  • Short-Run Phillips Curve (SRPC): Downward sloping. Trade-off between inflation and unemployment. (Shifts in AD cause movements ALONG the SRPC; shifts in SRAS shift the ENTIRE SRPC).
  • Long-Run Phillips Curve (LRPC): Vertical line at the Natural Rate of Unemployment. No trade-off in the long run.

19. Foreign Exchange (FOREX) Market

Where currencies are traded. Exchange rates determine the price of imports and exports.

  • Appreciation: Currency gains value. (Makes exports expensive, imports cheap).
  • Depreciation: Currency loses value. (Makes exports cheap, imports expensive).
  • Shifters: Changes in Tastes, Relative Incomes, Relative Price Levels, and Relative Interest Rates.

20. The Ample Reserves Framework

A modern framework where the banking system has abundant reserves. The Fed uses interest rates directly rather than shifting money supply.

  • Interest on Reserve Balances (IORB): The primary tool. The Fed adjusts this rate to steer the Federal Funds Rate.
  • Monetary Policy Action: To cool inflation, the Fed raises the IORB. To fix a recession, the Fed lowers the IORB.
