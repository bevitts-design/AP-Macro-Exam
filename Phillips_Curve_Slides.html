1. Introduction to the Phillips Curve

The Phillips Curve is a foundational macroeconomic model that illustrates the relationship between two key economic indicators:

  • Inflation Rate: Plotted on the vertical (Y) axis.
  • Unemployment Rate: Plotted on the horizontal (X) axis.

It helps us visualize the trade-offs policymakers face when trying to stabilize the economy.

2. The Short-Run Phillips Curve (SRPC)

The SRPC demonstrates an inverse relationship (trade-off) between inflation and unemployment in the short run.

  • When unemployment is high, inflation tends to be low.
  • When unemployment is low, inflation tends to be high.

3. The Long-Run Phillips Curve (LRPC)

In the long run, there is no trade-off between inflation and unemployment.

  • Shape: Vertical line anchored at the economy's Natural Rate of Unemployment (NRU).
  • Intersection: The exact point where SRPC and LRPC intersect represents the expected rate of inflation.

4. The Golden Rule: Movements vs. Shifts

Knowing what causes a dot to slide along the curve vs. what shifts the entire curve is the most tested concept in Unit 5.

1. Movement ALONG the SRPC: Caused by a change in Aggregate Demand (AD).

2. SHIFT of the entire SRPC: Caused by a change in Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS) OR a change in Inflationary Expectations.

  • AD Increases: Unemployment falls, inflation rises → Movement UP and LEFT along SRPC.
  • AD Decreases: Unemployment rises, inflation falls → Movement DOWN and RIGHT along SRPC.

5. Shifting the SRPC (Stagflation)

Changes in Aggregate Supply (SRAS) cause the SRPC to shift in the opposite direction.

  • Negative Supply Shock (SRAS ↓): Creates stagflation. SRPC shifts to the RIGHT (higher inflation AND higher unemployment).
  • Positive Supply Shock (SRAS ↑): SRPC shifts to the LEFT.

6. Shifting the SRPC (Expectations)

The second cause of a shift in the SRPC is a change in Inflationary Expectations.

  • If consumers and businesses expect higher inflation in the future, they demand higher wages now.
  • This increases costs for businesses, shifting SRAS left.
  • As a result, the SRPC shifts UP (to the right).
  • If expected inflation falls, the SRPC shifts DOWN (to the left).

7. Long-Run Adjustment Process

AP FRQs love to ask how the economy self-adjusts over time. Let's trace an Inflationary Gap scenario:

  1. Govt uses expansionary policy → AD shifts right.
  2. Unemployment falls → Economy moves UP/LEFT along the SRPC.
  3. Over time: People adjust their expectations to the new, higher inflation level.
  4. Workers demand higher wages → SRAS shifts left.
  5. The SRPC shifts UP/RIGHT, returning the economy to the vertical LRPC (NRU), but permanently stuck with a higher inflation rate.

8. AP Exam Tips & Graphing Precision

Zero Point Danger: An unlabeled or mislabeled graph earns ZERO points on the FRQ!

  • Axes Labels: Must be "Inflation Rate" (Y) and "Unemployment Rate" (X). Do not use Price Level and Real GDP here!
  • Intersection: Clearly mark the intersection of SRPC and LRPC to show expected inflation.
  • Plot Numbers: If a prompt gives you specific numerical values (e.g., "Assume a 5% natural rate of unemployment"), you must label the LRPC at the 5% mark on the X-axis.
