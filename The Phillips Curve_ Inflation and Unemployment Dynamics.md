# The Phillips Curve: Inflation and Unemployment Dynamics

\*\*Study Guide: The Phillips Curve (Unit 5)\*\*

The Phillips Curve is a foundational macroeconomic model that illustrates the relationship between the \*\*inflation rate\*\* (plotted on the vertical axis) and the \*\*unemployment rate\*\* (plotted on the horizontal axis) \[1, 2\]. 

\*\*The Short-Run Phillips Curve (SRPC)\*\*
\*   \*\*Shape and Meaning:\*\* The SRPC \*\*slopes downward\*\*, demonstrating an \*\*inverse relationship\*\* (or trade-off) between inflation and unemployment in the short run \[1-3\].
\*   \*\*Movements Along the Curve:\*\* A change in Aggregate Demand (AD) causes a movement \*along\* the SRPC \[1\]. 
    \*   If AD increases (such as from expansionary fiscal policy), unemployment falls and inflation rises. This is represented as a movement \*\*up and to the left\*\* along the SRPC \[3, 4\].
    \*   If AD decreases, unemployment rises and inflation falls, causing a movement \*\*down and to the right\*\* along the SRPC.

\*\*The Long-Run Phillips Curve (LRPC)\*\*
\*   \*\*Shape and Meaning:\*\* The LRPC is a \*\*vertical line\*\* anchored at the economy's \*\*Natural Rate of Unemployment (NRU)\*\* (or full-employment output) \[1-3\]. Because it is vertical, it shows that in the long run, there is \*\*no trade-off\*\* between inflation and unemployment \[3, 5\].
\*   \*\*The Intersection:\*\* The exact point where the SRPC and the LRPC intersect represents the economy's \*\*expected rate of inflation\*\* \[1, 6\]. When the economy is in long-run equilibrium, the actual inflation rate will match this expected rate at the intersection \[1\]. If the economy is in a recession, the current point on the SRPC will be to the right of the LRPC \[3\].

\*\*Shifting the Short-Run Phillips Curve\*\*
While AD causes movement along the curve, the SRPC itself will shift due to two primary factors:
1.  \*\*Changes in Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS):\*\* Changes in AS cause the SRPC to shift in the opposite direction of the AS shift \[1\]. For example, a negative supply shock (like a spike in oil prices) decreases SRAS, causing stagflation. On the Phillips Curve model, this \*\*shifts the SRPC to the right\*\*, meaning the economy experiences both higher inflation and higher unemployment simultaneously \[3, 7\].
2.  \*\*Changes in Inflationary Expectations:\*\* If consumers and businesses expect higher inflation in the future, the \*\*SRPC shifts up (to the right)\*\* \[4\]. If expected inflation falls, the \*\*SRPC shifts down (to the left)\*\* \[4, 8\].

\*\*The Long-Run Adjustment Process\*\*
AP exam questions often ask you to connect the short-run trade-off to long-run self-adjustment \[4\]. 
\*   \*\*Inflationary Gap Scenario:\*\* If the government uses expansionary policy to lower unemployment, AD shifts right, and the economy moves up and left along the SRPC to a point of higher inflation \[4\]. Over time, people adjust their expectations to this new, higher inflation level. This causes the \*\*SRPC to shift upward\*\*, returning the economy's unemployment rate back to the NRU (the vertical LRPC) but leaving it permanently stuck with a higher inflation rate \[4\].
\*   \*\*Recessionary Gap Scenario:\*\* During a long-run self-adjustment from a recession, input prices and inflationary expectations eventually decrease, which causes the \*\*SRPC to shift to the left\*\* \[8\].

\*\*Key Tips for the AP Exam\*\*
\*   \*\*Memorize the Rule:\*\* \*\*Changes in AD = Movement along the SRPC\*\*. \*\*Changes in AS or Expectations = Shift of the SRPC\*\* \[1\].
\*   \*\*Graphing Precision:\*\* An unlabeled or mislabeled graph earns zero points on the FRQs \[9\]. Always label the axes correctly (Inflation Rate and Unemployment Rate), clearly mark the intersection as the expected inflation rate, and if a prompt gives you specific numerical values (e.g., 5% natural rate of unemployment), you must accurately plot those numbers on your graph \[6, 10\].
